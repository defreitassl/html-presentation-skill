Executive summary

WHO describes outdoor air pollution as a major environmental health problem affecting people in low-, middle-, and high-income countries. The burden is especially concentrated in lower-income settings, where exposure and health system constraints compound risk.

The source material is better served by a document-style presentation than by a dense dashboard because the central argument depends on context, policy levers, and health impacts rather than a single operational decision.

The strongest executive message is that air pollution is both a health issue and a cross-sector policy problem.

Transport, energy, industry, urban planning, waste management, and household energy all appear in WHO's policy framing, which makes governance coordination a core theme.

Analysis

The WHO fact sheet links ambient fine particulate exposure to cardiovascular and respiratory disease and cancers. It estimates that, in 2019, 68% of outdoor air pollution related premature deaths were due to ischaemic heart disease and stroke.

The policy implication is that air quality cannot be treated as an isolated environment file. It needs transport, energy, urban design, waste, household energy, and industrial policy coordination.

Health impact

WHO estimates 4.2 million premature deaths worldwide from ambient air pollution in 2019.

Exposure scale

WHO reports that 99% of the global population lived where guideline levels were not met in 2019.

Executive summary

The pilot tests operations before the city commits to permanent infrastructure.

The proposal keeps the first phase small enough to operate manually, but instrumented enough to prove whether the corridors deserve capital investment.

Focus on reliability

The north-south bus corridor is measured by delay, bunching, dwell time, and rider satisfaction rather than by infrastructure completion alone.

Use temporary tools

Paint, signage, staff assignments, and signal timing changes allow the city to learn before installing permanent curb or lane changes.

Decide with evidence

Weekly dashboards and rider feedback create a decision trail for extension, adjustment, or cancellation after twelve weeks.

Operating model

One pilot room coordinates streets, transit, data, and communications.

The pilot is treated as a service operation, not a one-time launch. Each week converts field observations into small changes.

RidersReport delays, crowding, safety issues, and confusing stops.
OperatorsSurface blocked stops, dwell-time friction, signal issues, and route conflicts.
MerchantsFlag delivery windows, curb access, and customer pickup concerns.
Pilot Room Weekly decisions, field notes, service metrics, and communications in one operating cadence.
Street changesTemporary markings, signage, curb rules, and enforcement support.
Service changesSignal timing requests, stop adjustments, and operator guidance.
Public updatesWeekly notices, pilot scorecards, and next-step explanations.

Service reliability

Track delay, bunching, travel time variation, and missed connections using existing transit telemetry.

Street performance

Measure blocked stops, curb occupancy, enforcement calls, and observed conflicts during peak windows.

Public sentiment

Collect rider and merchant feedback weekly with short forms and a published response log.

Mobility lead

Owns pilot cadence, board reporting, and weekly decision records.

Field coordinator

Coordinates signage, curb observations, operator notes, and issue escalation.

Data analyst

Maintains dashboard, compares baseline trends, and prepares the final recommendation memo.

Roadmap

Four phases move from setup to a board-ready recommendation.

The timeline avoids permanent construction commitments until the pilot has enough operational evidence.

01

Baseline and field setup

Confirm corridor baselines, install temporary markings, brief operators, and publish the pilot explainer.

02

Launch and stabilize

Run daily field checks, resolve confusing curb rules, and tune the first dashboard view.

03

Weekly operating adjustments

Review service metrics, rider notes, enforcement observations, and merchant feedback every Friday.

04

Recommendation package

Prepare a board memo with outcomes, tradeoffs, cost range, and options for extension or permanent design.

Risks and mitigations

The highest risks are operational clarity, public trust, and measurement quality.

Each risk has an owner and a visible mitigation path before launch.

Mitigation: publish simple loading windows, place field staff during the first week, and update signage if the same confusion repeats.
Mitigation: use four weeks of historical transit data and run two manual field counts before the pilot begins.
Mitigation: set a weekly merchant listening window and preserve documented delivery exceptions during the pilot.
Decision needed

Approve the operating pilot and authorize a final recommendation after twelve weeks.

The requested decision is not a permanent buildout. It is permission to run a measured pilot with clear public communication and a board review gate.

Approve scope

Three corridors, twelve weeks, temporary treatments, and weekly operating review.

Assign owners

Mobility, transit operations, street services, communications, and data reporting.

Set the review gate

Return with recommendation options: end, extend, revise, or convert to permanent design.
